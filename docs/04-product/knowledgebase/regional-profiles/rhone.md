# Rhône grape varieties: Comprehensive viticulture and winemaking profiles

Six classic Rhône varieties—**Syrah, Grenache, Mourvèdre** (reds) and **Viognier, Roussanne, Marsanne** (whites)—share Mediterranean climate adaptation but differ dramatically in phenology, disease profiles, and management requirements. This profile provides structured, queryable data for vineyard decision-making.

## Climate requirements and heat accumulation

All six varieties excel in warm, dry climates but have distinct temperature thresholds and growing degree day (GDD) requirements that determine site suitability.

| Variety | Optimal GDD (°C-days) | Winkler Region | Heat Requirement | Drought Tolerance |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Syrah** | 1,722 | III-IV | Moderate-High | Moderate-Good |
| **Grenache** | 1,693 | III-V | High | **High** |
| **Mourvèdre** | 1,945-2,222 | **IV (Hot)** | **Very High** | Moderate |
| **Viognier** | 1,400-1,950 | II-III | Moderate | Moderate-Good |
| **Roussanne** | 1,550-1,950 | II-III | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Marsanne** | 1,600-2,200 | II-III | Moderate | **Poor-Moderate** |

**Mourvèdre** demands the most heat—Bandol's growing season runs 5°C warmer than Châteauneuf-du-Pape for reliable ripening. **Grenache** tolerates the hottest conditions with excellent drought resistance, making both ideal for warming climates. The white varieties perform best in moderate heat zones where acidity retention remains possible.

### Critical temperature thresholds

| Variety | Photosynthesis Shutdown | Dormant LT50 | Bud Frost Damage |
|---------|------------------------|--------------|------------------|
| **Syrah** | >35°C (95°F) | -15 to -21°C | <0°C (32°F) |
| **Grenache** | >35°C (95°F) | -15 to -18°C | <0°C (32°F) |
| **Mourvèdre** | >38°C (100°F) | -15 to -18°C | <0°C (32°F) |
| **Viognier** | >38°C (100°F) | -15 to -18°C | **<-1°C (30°F)** |
| **Roussanne** | >35°C (95°F) | -15 to -18°C | <0°C (32°F) |
| **Marsanne** | >35°C (95°F) | -15 to -20°C | <0°C (32°F) |

All varieties classify as "tender" V. vinifera with similar dormant hardiness. **Viognier's early budbreak** creates heightened spring frost vulnerability—Tablas Creek lost crops to frost in 2001, 2009, 2011, and 2022.

## Phenology and ripening sequence

Understanding relative timing enables harvest planning and variety pairing across vineyard blocks.

### Budbreak order (earliest to latest)
1. **Viognier** (late March) — Early; high frost risk
2. **Grenache** (early April) — Early; wind-sensitive bloom
3. **Marsanne** (mid-March) — Late; 9 days after Chasselas
4. **Syrah** (mid-season) — 75-87 GDD to budbreak
5. **Roussanne** (late March-April) — Late; frost protection built-in
6. **Mourvèdre** (late) — 13 days after Chasselas; natural frost avoidance

### Harvest sequence (Rhône/California timing)

| Variety | Typical Harvest | Days Bloom→Harvest | Target Brix | Target pH |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Viognier** | Late Aug-early Sept | 100-115 | **≤24°** | 3.2-3.5 |
| **Marsanne** | Mid-September | 100-115 | **20-22°** | 3.3-3.5 |
| **Roussanne** | Late September | 105-120 | 21-24° | 3.2-3.6 |
| **Syrah** | Early-mid September | 100-115 | 24-26° | 3.5-3.7 |
| **Grenache** | Late September | 100-120 | **25-27°** | 3.3-3.45 |
| **Mourvèdre** | **Mid-Oct-Nov** | 115-130+ | 24-27° | 3.4-3.7 |

**Mourvèdre** ripens last, sometimes 4.5 weeks after reference varieties, requiring 180+ frost-free days. **Marsanne** uniquely ripens at low sugars (often 12% potential alcohol) while **Grenache** accumulates sugar rapidly, frequently reaching 25-27°Brix.

### Critical timing considerations

**Viognier's narrow window:** Flavor development occurs within days, not weeks—harvest timing separates "mean-spirited Sauvignon Blanc" from "voluptuous aromatics." **Grenache's bloom sensitivity:** Wind and cool temperatures (<15°C) during flowering cause severe shatter (coulure), making weather during this 1-2 week period crop-defining.

## Disease susceptibility matrix

Disease pressure varies dramatically across varieties, significantly impacting management intensity and chemical inputs.

| Variety | Powdery Mildew | Downy Mildew | Botrytis | Eutypa | Special Concerns |
|---------|---------------|--------------|----------|--------|------------------|
| **Syrah** | Moderate-High | Moderate | Moderate-High | Susceptible | Shiraz disease (viral); reduction tendency |
| **Grenache** | Moderate-High | Susceptible | Susceptible | Standard | **Oxidation-prone**; coulure/shatter |
| **Mourvèdre** | Moderate-High | Moderate-High | **Low** | Susceptible | **Mites, leafhoppers, esca** |
| **Viognier** | **High** | Moderate-High | Moderate | Standard | Powdery mildew is primary challenge |
| **Roussanne** | **Very High** | Moderate | **High** | Standard | "The princess"—highest disease pressure |
| **Marsanne** | High | Moderate-High | Moderate-High | Standard | Mite susceptibility |

**Roussanne** requires the most intensive spray program—Tablas Creek calls it "the princess" due to high powdery mildew and botrytis susceptibility combined with tight clusters and thin skins. **Mourvèdre's** thick skins provide botrytis resistance, advantageous for late-harvest situations, but mite and leafhopper monitoring is essential.

### Oxidation vs. reduction tendency (winemaking critical)

| Variety | Tendency | Management Implication |
|---------|----------|----------------------|
| **Grenache** | **Highly oxidation-prone** | Minimize air exposure; careful SO₂; early bottling |
| **Mourvèdre** | **Highly reductive** | Requires oxygen exposure; frequent racking; micro-ox beneficial |
| **Syrah** | Moderately reductive | Open-top fermenters; monitor H₂S |
| **Whites** | Standard | Protect from oxidation |

This oppositional chemistry makes Grenache and Mourvèdre complementary in GSM blends—Mourvèdre's need for oxygen exposure balances Grenache's oxidation sensitivity.

## Vineyard management profiles

### Vigor and training requirements

| Variety | Vigor | Preferred Training | Growth Habit | Crop Load (tons/acre) |
|---------|-------|-------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Syrah** | **High** | VSP, cordon | Horizontal (requires trellising) | 2-4 (premium: 2-3) |
| **Grenache** | Moderate-High | **Gobelet/bush vine** | **Upright, self-supporting** | 1-3 (bush: 1-2) |
| **Mourvèdre** | Low-Moderate | Gobelet, head | **Upright** | **<2.5** |
| **Viognier** | Moderate-High | VSP | Standard | 2.5-3.5 |
| **Roussanne** | Low-Moderate | VSP | Erect | 2-4 (variable) |
| **Marsanne** | **High** | VSP, divided canopy | Standard | 2-4 |

**Syrah** is the only Rhône variety requiring trellising due to its unique horizontal growth habit—canes extend outward rather than upward, making it the only variety permitted trellised in gobelet-required Châteauneuf-du-Pape. **Grenache's** upright, rigid wood makes it ideal for traditional bush training, which also protects against wind damage during bloom.

### Canopy management intensity

| Variety | Shoot Thinning | Leaf Removal | Cluster Thinning | Overall Intensity |
|---------|---------------|--------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Syrah** | Required (3×) | Essential | Often required | **High** |
| **Grenache** | As needed | Moderate | Quality-dependent | Moderate |
| **Mourvèdre** | Avoid over-trimming | Caution (sunburn) | Rarely needed | Low-Moderate |
| **Viognier** | Required | Essential (disease) | Often required | **High** |
| **Roussanne** | Essential | **Aggressive** | As needed | **High** |
| **Marsanne** | Required | Essential | **Required** | **High** |

**Mourvèdre** requires minimal canopy intervention—its limited foliage and tendency toward sunburn mean excessive trimming harms quality. Conversely, **Roussanne** demands aggressive leaf thinning for disease control despite sun exposure needs.

## Rootstock recommendations by variety

Matching rootstock to variety vigor and site conditions significantly impacts wine quality.

### Red varieties

| Variety | Devigorating Options | Drought-Tolerant | Standard/Moderate |
|---------|---------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Syrah** (high vigor) | 420A, **Schwarzmann**, 161-49C, 3309C | 110R, 1103P | 101-14, SO4 |
| **Grenache** (moderate) | 101-14, 3309C, Riparia Gloire | **110R, 140Ru**, Malague 44-53 | SO4, 5C |
| **Mourvèdre** (low) | — | **110R**, 41B | 161-49C, 140Ru |

**Spanish research on Mourvèdre** found low-vigor rootstocks (110R, 161-49C) produced wines with higher polyphenols, anthocyanins, and color intensity. For **Syrah**, avoid Salt Creek (copper toxicity issues).

### White varieties

| Variety | Vigor Control Priority | Recommended | Drought-Tolerant |
|---------|----------------------|-------------|------------------|
| **Viognier** (moderate-high) | **High** | **Schwarzmann** (marked devigorating effect), 3309C, 420A | 110R (caution: vigor) |
| **Roussanne** (low-moderate) | Moderate | 101-14, 3309C, 420A | 110R, 1103P |
| **Marsanne** (high) | **High** | 3309C, 101-14, Schwarzmann | 110R, 1103P |

**Schwarzmann** shows a "marked devigorating effect on Viognier specifically" (Lodi Growers research), making it the preferred choice for vigor control.

## GSM blending: Contributions and ratios

The Grenache-Syrah-Mourvèdre blend leverages complementary characteristics from each variety.

### Component contributions

| Variety | Primary Contribution | Secondary | Sensory Impact |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Grenache** | Fruit, body, alcohol | Soft tannins, spice | Red fruits, warmth, glycerin |
| **Syrah** | **Color**, structure, tannin | Minerality, spice | Dark fruits, pepper, longevity |
| **Mourvèdre** | Structure, tannin, earthiness | Color, gamey complexity | Meaty, leathery, finish length |

### Traditional blend ratios

| Style | Grenache | Syrah | Mourvèdre | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Southern Rhône standard** | 40-70% | 10-30% | 10-30% | Grenache-dominant |
| **Châteauneuf-du-Pape** | 50-80%+ | Secondary | ~6.6% plantings | Up to 19 varieties permitted |
| **Bandol red (AOC)** | Variable | Variable | **50-95%** | Mourvèdre minimum required |
| **Côtes du Rhône (AOC)** | Lead | — | — | GSM ≥60% of wine |

### Viognier co-fermentation with Syrah (Côte-Rôtie tradition)

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Legal maximum** | 20% Viognier |
| **Typical practice** | 5-10% |
| **Requirement** | Must be **co-fermented** (not blended post-fermentation) |
| **Effect at 5%** | No perceivable color difference |
| **Effect at 10-20%** | Aromatic lift; potential color dilution |
| **Chemistry** | Viognier flavanols conjugate with Syrah anthocyanins, shifting color toward purple/blue |

**Technique:** Destem and crush Viognier on top of destemmed Syrah before fermentation begins. Viognier skins provide the flavanols needed for color stabilization.

### White Rhône blend compositions

Traditional Northern Rhône white blends use **Marsanne (80-85%)** with **Roussanne (15-20%)** providing aromatic lift and aging potential. Southern Rhône whites incorporate Grenache Blanc, Clairette, Bourboulenc, and Picpoul alongside Roussanne and Marsanne.

## Flavor profiles across climate zones

Climate dramatically shapes wine character, particularly for these heat-loving varieties.

### Red varieties by climate

| Variety | Cool Climate | Moderate Climate | Warm/Hot Climate |
|---------|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Syrah** | **White pepper** (rotundone), blackcurrant, olive, nervy acidity | Blackberry, plum, black pepper, smoke | Jammy blueberry, chocolate, tar; 14-15.5% ABV |
| **Grenache** | Lighter body, strawberry, cherry, herbal; Pinot-like | Red/dark fruits, garrigue herbs, cinnamon | Rich black cherry, fig; **14.5-16%+ ABV** |
| **Mourvèdre** | *Struggles*—green, harsh tannins | Cherry, medium body; better for blending | **Meaty, gamey**, blackberry, leather; deep purple |

**Syrah's peppery character** comes from rotundone, which develops in cool climates with late harvest, high vigor, and ample water. **Grenache** naturally accumulates high sugar regardless of climate, making alcohol management crucial.

### White varieties by climate

| Variety | Cool Climate | Moderate Climate | Warm/Hot Climate |
|---------|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Viognier** | Citrus, white flowers, leaner | **Classic:** Peach, apricot, violets | Tropical, low acid, heavy; requires intervention |
| **Roussanne** | Citrus, green tea, mineral | Stone fruit, honeysuckle, herbal tea | Lower acid, fuller; earlier picking essential |
| **Marsanne** | Fresh citrus, mineral | **Optimal:** Honeysuckle, melon, honey | Tropical, flabby; muted flavors |

**Viognier** develops dramatically different character based on harvest timing within a matter of days—early harvest produces "mean-spirited" wines while optimal timing achieves aromatic complexity.

## Winemaking parameters

### Target harvest chemistry

| Variety | Brix | pH | TA (g/L) | Special Notes |
|---------|------|-----|----------|---------------|
| **Syrah** | 24-26° | 3.5-3.7 | 5.5-6.0 | Monitor berry shriveling above 21-22°Brix |
| **Grenache** | 25-27° | 3.3-3.45 | 6-7.5 | Sugar accumulates rapidly; harvest promptly |
| **Mourvèdre** | 24-27° | 3.4-3.7 | 5-7 | High-pH variety; acid addition often needed |
| **Viognier** | **≤24°** | 3.2-3.5 | **7+** | Narrow window; flavor over numbers |
| **Roussanne** | 21-24° | 3.2-3.6 | 6-7 | Harvest before acidity drops |
| **Marsanne** | **20-22°** | 3.3-3.5 | 5.5-7 | Naturally low sugar; ripens early |

### Fermentation recommendations

| Variety | Temp Range | Skin Contact | Whole Cluster | Oak Preference |
|---------|-----------|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Syrah** | 75-85°F (24-30°C) | 7-21 days | Up to 60% (cool); 20-30% (warm) | French or American; 25-35% new |
| **Grenache** | **65-75°F (18-24°C)** | Extended (color extraction) | Minimal | **Neutral/large format** |
| **Mourvèdre** | 75-86°F (24-30°C) | ~10 days | Traditional in Bandol | **Neutral only** |
| **Viognier** | **50-60°F (10-15°C)** | None recommended | N/A | Neutral strongly preferred |
| **Roussanne** | 55-65°F (13-18°C) | Minimal | N/A | Mixed: foudre + stainless |
| **Marsanne** | 54-64°F (12-18°C) | Minimal | N/A | Neutral or stainless |

**Grenache** requires cool fermentation to preserve delicate aromatics—hot fermentation causes herbaceous, coarse character. **Mourvèdre** does not absorb oak flavors well, making neutral oak or large format essential.

### Oxygen management during élevage

| Variety | Oxygen Need | Management |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **Mourvèdre** | **High** | Frequent pump-overs, racking, micro-oxygenation (20-60 mg/L/month pre-MLF) |
| **Syrah** | Moderate | Open-top fermenters; monitor reduction |
| **Grenache** | **Minimal** | Protect from air; top barrels frequently; inert gas |
| **Whites** | Low | Standard protective handling |

### Aging potential

| Variety | Standard Quality | Premium | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|---------|-------|
| **Syrah** | 5-10 years | 15-20+ years | Gains leather, truffle with age |
| **Grenache** | 2-5 years | 10-20+ years | Old vine wines age best |
| **Mourvèdre** | 8-12 years | **15+ years** | 4-8 year "closed period" |
| **Viognier** | **1-3 years** | 3-5 years | Terpenes oxidize; drink young |
| **Roussanne** | 8-15 years | **15-20+ years** | **Superior aging** among whites |
| **Marsanne** | 3-5 years | 15-25+ years | Develops hazelnut, caramel |

**Roussanne** offers the greatest aging potential among white Rhône varieties—Beaucastel Roussanne Vieilles Vignes ages for generations. **Viognier** should generally be bottled within 4-6 months and consumed young to preserve volatile terpenes.

## Small-scale growing recommendations

### Beginner accessibility ranking

| Rank | Variety | Rating | Key Considerations |
|------|---------|--------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Grenache** | **Moderate** | Drought/heat tolerant; wind sensitivity at bloom; oxidation in cellar |
| 2 | **Marsanne** | **Moderate-Good** | Easier than Roussanne; vigor management needed |
| 3 | **Syrah** | **Moderate** | High vigor; horizontal growth complicates training |
| 4 | **Viognier** | **Moderate-Difficult** | Powdery mildew; harvest timing critical |
| 5 | **Roussanne** | **Difficult** | "The princess"—high disease pressure |
| 6 | **Mourvèdre** | **Not Recommended** | Requires hottest sites; may not ripen |

### Advantages for home growers

**Heat/drought tolerance:** Grenache, Syrah, and Viognier handle dry conditions better than most V. vinifera, reducing irrigation requirements. **Bush training option:** Grenache and Mourvèdre's upright growth allows gobelet training without trellis infrastructure. **Versatility:** Grenache makes excellent rosé, providing an earlier-drinking product while red wines age.

### Variety-specific challenges

- **Grenache:** Shatter risk requires wind protection during bloom; oxidation demands careful cellar work
- **Syrah:** Three-pass thinning typical; horizontal shoots require positioning
- **Mourvèdre:** May fail to ripen outside hot microclimates; multiple harvest passes needed
- **Viognier:** 10-14 day spray intervals mandatory; harvest window measured in days
- **Roussanne:** Full powdery mildew and botrytis programs required; erratic yields
- **Marsanne:** Constant pruning regime; crop thinning essential for quality

### Recommended first Rhône variety for beginners

**Grenache** offers the best balance of climate resilience and forgiving viticulture, though winemaking requires attention to oxidation management. For white varieties, **Marsanne** provides more consistent production and better disease resistance than Roussanne, though both demand more spray program diligence than hybrid alternatives.
