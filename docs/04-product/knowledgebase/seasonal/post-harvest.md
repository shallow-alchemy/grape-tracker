# Post-harvest vineyard management: A phenological guide for small-scale growers

**The work you do between harvest and dormancy directly determines next year's crop quality, vine health, and winter survival.** This often-neglected period is when vines replenish the carbohydrate reserves that fuel spring growth, develop cold hardiness, and when disease management decisions affect next season's spray burden. Research shows high-yielding vines require **6-8 weeks of active leaf function after harvest** for full reserve restoration—making every post-harvest day count.

---

## Why the post-harvest period matters more than you think

The science is unequivocal: grapevines store **50-75% of their total carbohydrate reserves in the root system**, with concentrations ranging from 3-50% of root dry weight. These reserves follow a U-shaped pattern through the season—depleted during spring growth, then rebuilt primarily between harvest and leaf fall. Research by Bennett et al. demonstrated that early defoliation reduced root starch from **17% to just 1.5%** by budbreak, devastating the following season.

Vines with depleted reserves exhibit unmistakable symptoms the following spring: variable budbreak, slow shoot elongation, pale foliage, and reduced flower numbers per inflorescence—up to **25% fewer flowers** and **40% slower shoot growth**. In cold climates, the stakes are higher: carbohydrate storage directly affects cold hardiness, as soluble sugars serve as cryoprotectants during winter dormancy.

The practical implication is straightforward—keep leaves healthy and photosynthetically active as long as nature allows, while preparing the vineyard for winter.

## Stage 1: Immediate post-harvest (first 2-3 weeks after picking)

**Phenological trigger:** Fruit removal complete; leaves still green and functional.

This is the most critical window for reserve accumulation. The vine, suddenly unburdened from fruit demands, redirects all photosynthetic output toward storage. In warm regions, this period extends 1-4 months; in cooler climates, you may have only a few precious weeks before frost.

### Irrigation decisions

Post-harvest irrigation is essential—not optional. UC Davis research recommends **at least 10% of your seasonal irrigation** be applied after harvest. For small vineyards, this translates to **10-20 gallons per vine**, applied to relieve stress and maintain the functional canopy. The goal is supporting photosynthesis without pushing new growth.

**What to do:** Resume irrigation within days of harvest if vines show any stress (wilting, leaf curl). Irrigate deeply to encourage root growth—this is actually a period of rapid root development. In Mediterranean climates, apply 4 acre-inches over September-October. In humid Eastern regions where rainfall is typical, supplement only during dry spells.

**Common mistake:** Cutting irrigation completely after harvest. Entering dormancy with a dry soil profile contributes to Delayed Spring Growth syndrome—uneven budbreak and reduced vigor the following season.

### Nutrition: The potassium window

Potassium uptake diminishes greatly **about 30 days after harvest**, making immediate post-harvest the optimal time for K fertilization. Each ton of grapes removes approximately **5 pounds of potassium**—a significant drain that must be replaced.

**What to do:** For fertigation systems, apply potassium nitrate (avoiding high-ammonium fertilizers) within two weeks of harvest. For dry applications, band potassium sulfate in a 6-8 inch deep furrow, 18-24 inches from the vine on each side. NPK blends like 3-12-14 are appropriate—note the low nitrogen, higher phosphorus and potassium ratio.

**The nitrogen caution—especially in cold climates:** While post-harvest is an efficient time for nitrogen uptake (approximately 30% of seasonal N can be applied now), **late nitrogen delays lignification and reduces cold hardiness**. In regions where winter survival matters, cease nitrogen applications by mid-summer. If you must apply nitrogen post-harvest, do so immediately after picking when weeks of warm weather remain, using nitrate forms for rapid uptake.

### Disease management begins now

Post-harvest fungicide applications serve two purposes: protecting leaves for continued photosynthesis, and reducing overwintering inoculum for next season. Research from Cornell demonstrates vineyards that continued powdery mildew sprays through Labor Day had **28 times fewer overwintering structures** than those stopping in July.

**For powdery mildew:** JMS Stylet Oil provides excellent eradicant activity on visible colonies. Potassium bicarbonate products (0-day PHI) are safe options. The goal is preventing cleistothecia formation—the black fruiting bodies that harbor the pathogen through winter in bark crevices.

**For trunk disease prevention—critical timing:** UC Davis IPM emphasizes completing sanitation activities "well before the first fall rain event." Spores release during rain and infect pruning wounds. Remove and destroy any cankered wood now, cutting 4-12 inches beyond visible infection into clean tissue. Do not leave diseased wood in row middles—it sporulates for years.

## Stage 2: Active leaf senescence (leaves changing color)

**Phenological trigger:** Chlorophyll breakdown visible; leaves transitioning from green to yellow, with some varieties showing red coloration.

This stage signals the vine's internal preparation for dormancy. Nutrients are being actively reabsorbed from leaves into permanent structures. In cold climates, this period is **critical for cold acclimation**—the vine is responding to shortening days and cooling temperatures by reducing tissue water content and accumulating cryoprotectants.

### Supporting the final push

**What to do:** Maintain disease protection to prevent premature defoliation. Even at this late stage, powdery and downy mildew infections disrupt carbohydrate export from leaves. Phosphorus acid fungicides (0-day PHI) provide downy mildew protection; sulfur products remain effective for powdery mildew.

**For irrigation:** In irrigated regions, continue light irrigation to maintain leaf function without delaying dormancy. In cold climates, gradually reduce irrigation to mimic natural environmental cues that trigger acclimation. The vine interprets moderate water stress as a signal to prepare for winter.

### Vineyard cleanup begins

**Remove mummified fruit** from vines—this is the single most important sanitation step for black rot control. Mummies hanging in the trellis are far more problematic than those on the ground. During this stage, also remove any second crop clusters and obviously diseased fruit.

**Cover crop decisions:** If planting a winter cover crop, seed after harvest when irrigation is available or rain expected. In California, seed late September through mid-October with 1 inch of irrigation to ensure germination. Vigorous vineyards should use cover crop mixes without legumes to avoid adding nitrogen; weaker vineyards benefit from 20-30% legume blends.

## Stage 3: Leaf fall begins

**Phenological trigger:** Abscission zone formation; leaves dropping naturally; partial canopy remaining.

As leaves fall, the vine's carbohydrate accumulation capacity diminishes. This is the transition point—management shifts from supporting photosynthesis to preparing for dormancy.

### Cold climate preparations

In regions where winter temperatures threaten vine survival, this is the window for protective measures. Cold hardiness in V. vinifera varieties develops progressively—MSU Extension data shows Chardonnay acclimating from approximately 2°F tolerance in mid-November to -11°F to -13°F by mid-January.

**Hilling up graft unions:** Create a soil mound **3-4 inches above the graft union** in late fall before the ground freezes. Research shows temperatures under 5 inches of hilled soil remained around 21°F when air temperature reached -18°F. This is essential protection for the graft union—the most vulnerable point on the vine.

**For extreme cold regions (Minnesota, parts of Michigan):** Cane burial protects vines where temperatures regularly drop below -15°F. After leaf fall, carefully remove vines from the trellis, pin canes to the ground, and cover with 4-6 inches of soil or straw. One Michigan grower using this technique achieved 60-75% normal crop after a polar vortex that devastated unprotected vines.

### Pest habitat reduction

**Rodent control is time-sensitive.** Voles girdle trunk bark below the soil line during winter, with damage often invisible until spring—sometimes killing young vines entirely. Before snow cover arrives:

- Mow vegetation short around vine trunks
- Maintain a bare strip (6-12 inches) around vine bases
- Remove debris piles and brush that provide habitat
- Consider zinc phosphide bait in active runways (restricted use pesticide, requires certified applicator)
- Install hardware cloth cylinders (¼-inch mesh) around individual trunks, buried 6 inches below soil and extending above anticipated snow depth

**Deer protection:** Install fencing before deer establish feeding patterns—waiting until damage occurs means the pattern is already set. Minimum effective height is 8 feet for permanent fencing. For small-scale growers, individual vine protection using wire mesh cylinders (6+ feet tall) may be more practical.

## Stage 4: Complete leaf fall and early dormancy

**Phenological trigger:** All leaves dropped; canes hardened with mature brown periderm; temperatures consistently cool.

The vine is now dormant, but management continues. This is the optimal time for infrastructure work and planning.

### Trellis and infrastructure assessment

**What to do:** Walk every row, inspecting posts, wires, and anchors. Make notes of needed repairs but defer most work until the dormant pruning period—you'll be in the vineyard anyway. Exception: fix anything that threatens vine support through winter winds and ice loading.

**Irrigation system winterization:** Drain all lines and components before hard freezes. Compressed air blow-out is essential for drip systems. Disconnect backflow preventers and store indoors.

### Trunk borer assessment

Borer damage is most visible during dormancy when foliage doesn't obscure entry holes. Examine old pruning scars and dead wood for round holes (~1/8 inch diameter), brown frass, and fine sawdust. Cut suspicious wood to reveal internal galleries.

**Key timing:** Remove all infested wood before adult emergence in spring (March-May). Do not leave prunings near the vineyard—remove and destroy, or chop and incorporate into soil before adults emerge.

### Record keeping and planning

**Document now while fresh:**
- Yield by block/variety and actual picking dates
- Disease pressure observations (which blocks, which diseases, when)
- Vine vigor assessments (pruning weights will provide more data)
- Weather events that affected the crop
- Spray program effectiveness

**Plan for next season:**
- Calculate vine balance from yield and pruning weight data
- Identify blocks needing vigor adjustment
- Order dormant spray materials (lime sulfur reduces overwintering powdery mildew by 30-50%)
- Schedule late-winter pruning (prune as late as possible to reduce trunk disease infection risk)

## Stage 5: Transition to full dormancy

**Phenological trigger:** Sustained temperatures below 45°F; no active growth; fully lignified canes.

The vine has completed acclimation and entered deep dormancy. Management now focuses on monitoring and protecting through winter.

### Young vine considerations

First through third-year vines are more sensitive to cold than mature vines. Their smaller root systems store fewer reserves, and they haven't developed the structural resilience of established plants.

**Additional protection for young vines:**
- Apply 2-4 inches of mulch (straw, shredded bark) around the base, leaving a gap at the trunk to prevent rot
- Ensure soil mounding covers the graft union plus a safety margin
- Consider trunk guards that also deter rodents
- Deep water before soil freezes to ensure adequate root-zone moisture through winter

**Training decisions before full dormancy:** Assess the season's growth and determine if training is on schedule. Flag vines needing additional attention during dormant pruning. In cold climates, maintaining multiple trunks ("spare parts viticulture") provides insurance—if one trunk is damaged, the survivor becomes the bearing structure.

### Winter monitoring

In cold climate regions, monitor bud hardiness through the winter using differential thermal analysis or controlled freezing tests (services available through university extension programs). This data informs spring pruning decisions—if significant bud damage has occurred, adjust pruning to leave more buds.

## Regional timing summary

Rather than calendar dates, use phenological observations:

- **In Mediterranean climates:** Expect 2-4 months of post-harvest leaf function. Disease pressure is typically lower in fall. Main concerns are irrigation management and trunk disease sanitation before winter rains.

- **In humid Eastern climates:** Post-harvest period may be only weeks. Disease management through September is critical for both protecting leaves and reducing next-year inoculum. Crown gall is a persistent concern—hilling helps protect infection sites.

- **In cold climates (Minnesota, Michigan, Finger Lakes):** Cold hardiness preparation dominates. Cease nitrogen by mid-summer. Hill graft unions. Consider cane burial for vinifera in extreme zones. Monitor bud hardiness through winter. Late pruning (March-April) reduces trunk disease infection when temperatures have remained below freezing for several weeks post-cut.

---

## Conclusion: Investment in next year's crop

The post-harvest period tests the disciplined grower. With fruit safely fermented, attention naturally wanders. But the research is clear: vines neglected now pay the price next season through reduced fruitfulness, weakened cold hardiness, and increased disease pressure.

For small-scale growers with limited time, prioritize in this order: **maintain leaf health as long as possible** (irrigation, disease control), **remove disease inoculum** (mummies, infected wood, debris), **protect graft unions** (hilling in cold climates), and **control rodents** before snow arrives. Every other task can wait for the dormant season.

The most common mistake is assuming the season ends at harvest. In reality, harvest merely shifts the vine's focus from fruit production to survival—and the grower's task from harvest to preservation.
