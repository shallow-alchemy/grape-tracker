# Petite Sirah (Durif) Varietal Profile

## Quick Reference

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Species** | Vitis vinifera |
| **Synonyms** | Durif (official), Petite Sirah (TTB spelling) |
| **Parentage** | Syrah × Peloursin (confirmed UC Davis 1997) |
| **Berry Color** | Black (small berries, thick skins) |
| **Primary Regions** | California (Lodi, Paso Robles, Napa, Sonoma), Australia |
| **Winkler Region** | III, IV, lower V |
| **GDD Requirement** | 2,500–3,500°F-days |
| **Vigor** | Moderate |
| **Yield Potential** | 3-7 tons/acre (premium: 3-4) |
| **Wine Styles** | Dry red (varietal or blend), Port-style |

## Climate Requirements

### Heat Accumulation

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Optimal GDD** | 2,500–3,500°F-days | Winkler Regions III-IV |
| **Minimum GDD** | ~2,000°F-days | Region III minimum |
| **Maximum GDD** | 4,900°F-days | Upper Region V tolerated |
| **Suitable Winkler regions** | III, IV, lower V | "All but very warmest/coolest" |

### Temperature Thresholds

| Condition | Temperature | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Photosynthesis optimal | 77-95°F (25-35°C) | Normal function |
| Stress onset | >95°F (35°C) | Acclimation activates |
| Significant reduction | >104°F (40°C) | ~60% photosynthesis reduction |
| Severe stress | >113°F (45°C) | Major PSII damage |

### Frost Tolerance

| Stage | LT50 Temperature |
|-------|------------------|
| Mid-winter dormancy | -5°F to 0°F (-20°C to -18°C) |
| Green swollen buds | 26°F (-3.3°C) |
| Bud burst | 28°F (-2.2°C) |
| Two-leaf stage | 29°F (-1.7°C) |

### Drought Tolerance

**Rating:** HIGH

- Originally bred for drought tolerance
- "Comparatively well adapted to water stress" (Lodi Winegrape Commission)
- Pair with drought-tolerant rootstocks (110R, 1103P, 140Ru) for enhanced performance
- Deep rooting when established

## Phenology

### Developmental Timing

| Stage | Timing | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **Bud break** | Late March–mid-April | Mid-season (not early) |
| **Bloom** | May | 40-80 days after bud break |
| **Veraison** | Late July–August | ~50 days after fruit set |
| **Harvest** | Late September–November | LATE ripening |

### GDD Thresholds

| Stage | GDD from Bud Break |
|-------|-------------------|
| Bloom | 240-372°F-days |
| Veraison | 556-800°F-days from bloom |
| Harvest | Variable; late-season |

### Ripening Classification

**LATE RIPENING** — One of the last varieties harvested

- 2-4 weeks later than Cabernet Sauvignon
- Late ripening creates frost risk in cooler years/sites
- Extended hang time tolerated (thick skins protect berries)

### Hang Time Tolerance

**Rating:** GOOD

- Small berries and thick skins allow extended hang time
- Risk: Berry shrivel at high Brix (>25°) with sun exposure
- Suitable for late-harvest/port-style wines

## Disease Susceptibility

### Disease Matrix

| Disease | Susceptibility | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| **Powdery mildew** | SLIGHT/RESISTANT | Originally bred for resistance |
| **Downy mildew** | RESISTANT | Key reason for original development |
| **Botrytis bunch rot** | HIGH | Tight clusters susceptible |
| **Trunk/cordon diseases** | HIGH | Eutypa, Esca—careful pruning required |
| **Phomopsis** | HIGH | Important management issue |
| **Phylloxera** | SUSCEPTIBLE | Requires resistant rootstock |

### Disease Resistance Advantages

- Developed in 1880s specifically for powdery mildew resistance
- High skin-to-juice ratio provides natural protection
- Dense pigmentation indicates high phenolic content
- Small berries = thicker relative skin proportion

### Botrytis Management (Critical)

**Challenge:** Tight clusters trap moisture

**Management strategies:**
- Gibberellin application to loosen clusters
- Well-aerated fruit zone through canopy management
- Sanitation essential
- Clone selection where available

## Vineyard Management

### Vigor Profile

| Attribute | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| **Vigor level** | Moderate |
| **Growth habit** | Draping, willowy (like parent Syrah) |
| **Fruitfulness** | HIGH—partitions large growth portion into fruit |
| **Critical issue** | Prone to OVERCROPPING |

**Warning:** Overcropping can lead to vine decline; cluster thinning essential

### Training Systems

| System | Suitability | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| **Wye trellis** | RECOMMENDED | Best for draping varieties |
| **VSP (modified)** | SUITABLE | Retain single leaf layer on south side |
| **T-top VSP** | GOOD | 24" T-trellis provides shade |
| **Bilateral cordon** | STANDARD | Limit cordon length by soil depth |
| **Head training** | TRADITIONAL | Old vine blocks |

### Vine Spacing

| Soil Type | Row × Vine Spacing | Vines/Acre |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|
| Deep loamy | 10-12' × 6-8' | 454-726 |
| Moderate | 8-10' × 6' | 726-907 |
| Restrictive | 8' × 4-6' | 907-1,360 |

### Crop Load Targets

| Quality Goal | Tons/Acre | Lbs/Vine |
|--------------|-----------|----------|
| Premium | 3-4 | 10-15 |
| High-end varietal | 5-6 | 15-22 |
| Commercial | 6-7 | 20-30 |

**Critical:** "Young vines produce way too much fruit" — cluster thinning essential from year one

### Canopy Management

| Practice | Priority | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Shoot thinning** | HIGH | Adjust to 1 shoot per spur early season |
| **Cluster thinning** | CRITICAL | Reduce to ~1 cluster per shoot |
| **Leaf pulling** | CAUTION | Berry shrivel with sun exposure; retain single leaf layer |
| **Hedging** | HIGH | Required for draping growth habit |

## Rootstock Recommendations

### For Vigor Control (Important)

| Rootstock | Vigor Level | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **420A** | LOW | Used in UC Davis PS trials; delays ripening |
| **101-14 Mgt** | LOW-MODERATE | Good phylloxera resistance |
| **3309C** | MODERATE | Requires virus-free scion |
| **Riparia Gloire** | VERY LOW | Very low vigor; early ripening |

### For Drought Tolerance

| Rootstock | Drought Rating | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **110R** | HIGH | Excellent drought tolerance, high vigor |
| **1103P** | HIGH | Good for deficit irrigation |
| **140Ru** | HIGHEST | Best water transport under prolonged stress |

### Recommended Combinations

| Condition | Rootstock Choice |
|-----------|------------------|
| Cool climate, fertile soil | 420A (low vigor, delays ripening) |
| Warm climate, deep soil | 110R or 1103P (drought-tolerant) |
| Hot climate, limited water | 140Ru (excellent drought tolerance) |
| Vigor control priority | 101-14 Mgt or Riparia Gloire |

## Winemaking Profile

### Harvest Chemistry Targets

| Parameter | Target Range | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Brix** | 24–26° | Some prefer 25°+ for full extraction |
| **pH** | 3.6–3.9 | Higher end common |
| **Pre-ferment TA** | 7.5–8.0 g/L | Adjust from typical 4.0-7.0 g/L |
| **Finished TA** | ~6.5 g/L | MLF will reduce acid |

### Fermentation Parameters

| Temperature | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| 70-85°F (21-29°C) | Recommended range |
| 81-86°F (27-30°C) | Traditional style peak |
| ~88°F (31°C) | "Cooler" for nuanced style |
| <90°F | Avoid off-aromas |

### Skin Contact (Critical)

**Note:** "Color almost immediately begins to release from the skins" at crush

| Duration | Result |
|----------|--------|
| 5-7 days | Standard, balanced extraction |
| 6 days | "Elegant sophistication, well-integrated tannins" |
| 11 days | Maximum extraction, big structure |
| Press at 1-2°B | Softer tannins, fruit-forward style |
| Press at 0°B (dry) | Full extraction |

### Oak Treatment

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Oak type** | American traditional; French for subtlety |
| **Duration** | 12-18 months standard; 17-20 premium |
| **New oak percentage** | 20-30% typical |

**Critical:** "Aging minimum two years is critical—tannin linking and elongation very noticeable"

### Tannin Management Techniques

1. Early pressing (before dryness) for softer style
2. Gentle punch-downs (3× daily)
3. Avoid enzymes if softer style desired
4. Egg white fining after 10-12 months
5. Extended barrel aging
6. Blending with softer varieties

### Aging Potential

| Category | Aging Window |
|----------|--------------|
| Entry-level | 5-7 years |
| Quality producer | 10-20 years |
| Premium (Turley, Ridge) | 25-40+ years |

## Blending Role

### What Petite Sirah Contributes

| Attribute | Level | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Color** | EXCEPTIONAL | One of darkest wine grapes |
| **Tannin** | VERY HIGH | Adds structure and backbone |
| **Structure** | HIGH | Provides framework |
| **Aging potential** | HIGH | Extends cellar life |
| **Acidity** | MEDIUM-HIGH | Helps balance fruit-forward blends |

### Typical Blend Percentages

| Application | Percentage | Notes |
|-------------|------------|-------|
| In Zinfandel | 5-25% | 10-20% optimal; >20% may overwhelm Zin character |
| In Cabernet | 5-15% | Intensifies color, boosts tannin |
| Petite Petit style | 85% PS / 15% Petit Verdot | Popular modern blend |

### Why Winemakers Use Petite Sirah

1. **Color insurance** — Deepens color reliably
2. **Structure builder** — Adds tannin backbone
3. **Consistency tool** — Maintains house style
4. **Age-worthiness** — Extends cellar life
5. **Economics** — Often less expensive than Cabernet

### Single Varietal Viability

**Rating:** YES — Increasingly popular

- Deep inky color, full body
- Blueberry, blackberry, black pepper flavors
- Notable 100% producers: Ridge, Turley, Foppiano, Concannon, Robert Biale

## Flavor Profile by Climate

| Climate Zone | Characteristics |
|--------------|-----------------|
| **Cool** (Sonoma Coast, coastal Mendocino) | Red fruit, blackberry, plum; dark chocolate, espresso, mocha; firmer tannins, higher acidity; HIGHEST age-worthiness |
| **Moderate** (Sonoma inland, Paso Robles, Dry Creek) | Blackberry, blueberry, plum, dark cherry; black pepper, vanilla, chocolate, cedar; full but approachable tannins; EXCELLENT aging (10-20+ years) |
| **Warm/Hot** (Lodi, Amador, Central Valley) | Jammy blackberry, cooked/dried fruit, blueberry pie; black pepper, vanilla, baking spices; GENTLER, softer, more velvety tannins; maximum color intensity |

**Key insight:** "Petite Sirahs grown in Lodi generally yield more fruit-forward qualities and gentler tannin structures than those from fog-influenced coastal areas."

## Small-Scale Growing Notes

### Advantages

- Heat and drought tolerant
- Reliable intense color even in challenging vintages
- Some disease resistance (powdery/downy mildew)
- Tough skins for shipping/handling
- Fruitful at basal nodes; easy spur pruning
- Excellent blending component

### Challenges

- Overcropping tendency; cluster thinning essential from year one
- Bunch rot from compact clusters in humid conditions
- Intense color stains everything (equipment, hands, clothes)
- Rapid tannin extraction requires monitoring
- Late ripening creates frost risk in cool climates

### Beginner Accessibility

**Rating:** MODERATE-GOOD

Good choice for warm, dry climates where late ripening isn't problematic. Cluster thinning discipline is essential—the biggest mistake is allowing overcrop. Disease resistance simplifies spray programs compared to many varieties.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Overcropping is THE signature challenge** — Young vines produce excessive fruit; cluster thinning essential to prevent vine decline

2. **Exceptional color extraction** — One of darkest wine grapes; color releases immediately at crush

3. **Late ripening variety** — 2-4 weeks after Cabernet; frost risk in cool climates

4. **HIGH drought tolerance** — Originally bred for water stress adaptation; pairs well with deficit irrigation

5. **Disease resistant** — Powdery and downy mildew resistance simplifies spray programs

6. **Botrytis susceptible** — Tight clusters require management in humid conditions

7. **Tannin management critical** — Early pressing, gentle handling, extended aging all soften aggressive tannins

8. **Premium blending component** — 5-25% adds color, structure, aging potential to Zinfandel and other varieties

9. **Single varietal increasingly popular** — Quality producers demonstrate variety's standalone merit

10. **Minimum 2-year aging** — Tannin linking and elongation dramatically improve wine over time
